# B2B Channel-Partner Playbook — When the Audience Is the Adopter, Not the Investor

> Read this when `mode: b2b` is detected in the brief. B2B / channel-partner pitches are NOT VC pitches with the ask changed — they have different psychology, different metrics, and different closing dynamics.

---

## 1. B2B vs VC: structural differences

| Element | VC pitch | B2B / channel-partner pitch |
|---------|---------|------------------------------|
| Buyer | Equity investor | Operator / mall / EO / retailer |
| What they buy | A piece of the company | A deployed product on their venue |
| Decision time | Days to weeks | Often weeks to months (procurement cycles) |
| Decision filter | "Will this 10× my fund?" | "Will this make my P&L better in 12 months?" |
| Risk frame | Outcome risk (could fail to grow) | Operational risk (could break my venue) |
| Numbers that matter | TAM, SAM, SOM, LTV/CAC, growth rate | ROI, payback months, integration cost, support SLA |
| Proof they want | Traction logos, growth chart | Comparable venue case study with before/after |
| Pattern match | "Stripe for X" | "We're already running at [comparable named venue]" |
| Closing language | Round size, use of funds, milestones | Pilot scope, commercial terms, integration timeline, decision deadline |

The deck structure stays the same (10-slide Classic), but the **emphasis, examples, and closing slide** all shift.

---

## 2. The B2B "first 90 seconds" filter (different from VC's)

| # | B2B filter question | Pre-empted by |
|---|--------------------|----------------|
| B1 | "Will this make my venue's P&L visibly better in 12 months?" | Slide 1 ROI promise + slide 6 P&L math |
| B2 | "Has someone like me already done this and survived?" | Slide 4 named-venue comparable + slide 7 case study |
| B3 | "How much will this cost me in money + people + months?" | Slide 6 cost slide + slide 9 integration timeline |
| B4 | "Who do I call at 2 AM when something breaks?" | Slide 8 support SLA + named on-the-ground team |
| B5 | "How do I get out if it doesn't work?" | Slide 10 pilot scope (intentionally bounded; explicit exit terms) |

If any of B1–B5 isn't answered, the operator passes (often without saying so — they just don't return calls).

## 3. The B2B 10-slide template (Classic-10 + B2B emphasis)

| # | Slide | What carries the slide in B2B mode |
|---|-------|------------------------------------|
| 1 | Title / Vision | Product name + venue logo lock-up + ROI promise headline (e.g. `+15% GMV in 90 days, payback in 2.4 months`) |
| 2 | Problem | Operator's daily pain in a photograph (queue, broken POS, exhausted manager) — visceral over statistical |
| 3 | Solution | Before / after operator dashboard side-by-side |
| 4 | Comparable venues / traction | `Already running at: [Senayan City] [Lippo Mall Kemang] [Plaza Indonesia]` + GMV uplift % per venue |
| 5 | Product | Three pillars: POS + Wristband + Control Room — icon grid + one demo loop |
| 6 | ROI math | Operator P&L: cost vs uplift in IDR, payback months, sensitivity table |
| 7 | Case study | One venue's full before / after — named operator, named numbers, named obstacles overcome |
| 8 | Team & support | Founders + on-the-ground deployment team + 24/7 support SLA + escalation path + named account manager |
| 9 | Roadmap (operator-facing) | What value lands at YOUR venue this quarter (analytics agent → ads agent → social agent) |
| 10 | Pilot offer | Pilot scope · Commercial terms · Integration timeline · Decision deadline (specific date 4–6 weeks out) |

**Versus VC mode:** slides 4 (comparable venues, not market sizing), 6 (operator P&L, not unit economics), 7 (case study, not competition), 8 (support SLA, not team backgrounds), 10 (pilot offer, not round size).

## 4. ROI calculator slide structure (slide 6)

The B2B ROI slide is the deal-deciding slide. Get it right.

### Required elements

| Element | Why |
|---------|-----|
| `Without us` baseline column | Operator sees their current state honestly |
| `With us` projected column | Same metrics, projected delta |
| Delta callout | The number that triggers the decision |
| Payback period | The number that survives "I'll think about it" |
| Sensitivity row | The number that survives DD |
| Source line | Audit trail (`based on Senayan City pilot, 6 months data, Q4 2025`) |

### Worked example structure

```
WITHOUT INDUSIA          WITH INDUSIA              DELTA
Monthly GMV: Rp 1.7 B    Monthly GMV: Rp 1.95 B    +Rp 250 jt (+15%)
Cash leakage: 12%        Cash leakage: 0.5%        +Rp 195 jt
                         (closed-loop wristband)
No booth-level insight   Real-time booth dashboard +Rp 60 jt menu mix
                         (ads agent + analytics)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Net monthly impact:                                +Rp 505 jt
Indusia fee (Rp 50 jt + 1% GMV):                  -Rp 70 jt
NET P&L IMPROVEMENT:                              +Rp 435 jt / month

PAYBACK PERIOD: 2.4 months  (one-time integration cost Rp 1 M)

Sensitivity (varying GMV uplift): 
  10% uplift → payback 3.5 months
  15% uplift → payback 2.4 months
  20% uplift → payback 1.8 months

Source: Senayan City pilot Q4 2025, 6-month observed data.
```

### Anti-patterns (will fail DD)

- Round numbers everywhere (looks made-up)
- No sensitivity table (looks brittle)
- No source line (looks fabricated)
- Single uplift % only (operators will press for variance)
- No mention of integration cost (looks like hidden costs)

## 5. Risk-reversal slide elements

Operators are operationally risk-averse. The deck must surface — not hide — the risk-reversal mechanisms:

| Element | Where it appears |
|---------|------------------|
| Pilot scope intentionally bounded | Slide 10 (e.g. "1 venue, 90 days, no auto-renewal") |
| Performance guarantee / SLA | Slide 8 (e.g. "99.5% uptime; 1% revenue refund per percentage point below") |
| Early-exit terms | Slide 10 (e.g. "30-day notice termination during pilot, no penalty") |
| Free integration period | Slide 10 (e.g. "Setup + 14-day onboarding included; ongoing fee starts day 15") |
| Named support contacts | Slide 8 (account manager + escalation contact + WhatsApp group) |
| Reference customer call | Offered in closing remarks, not on the deck |

**B2B-specific rule:** never hide the cost in fine print. Surface it cleanly on slide 6 alongside the upside. Operators trust founders who name the cost upfront.

## 6. Case-study slide format (slide 7)

The case study is the deal-closer for skeptical operators. Structure:

```
[Venue name + logo + 1-line context]
e.g. "Senayan City — 47 tenant food court, ~Rp 2.3 B GMV/month"

[Before metrics — 3 specific numbers]
- Cash leakage observed: 14% (audited Q3 2025)
- Tenant churn: 18 of 47 tenants in 12 months
- Operator's main pain: weekly cash count took 6 hours

[The integration]
- Deployed: closed-loop wristband + tablet POS at all 47 booths
- Timeline: 12 days from contract to first transaction
- Disruption: zero — gradual cutover during off-peak hours

[After metrics — 3 specific numbers, same format]
- Cash leakage: 0.4% (audited Q1 2026)
- Tenant churn: 3 of 47 tenants in 6 months (annualized 13% — down from 38%)
- Operator's main relief: cash count is automated, instant Monday morning

[Operator quote — verbatim, attributed, dated]
"In month two we caught a tenant under-reporting by Rp 38 jt. The wristband data made it visible. We renegotiated, kept the tenant. That was the wristband paying for itself in one event."
— Pak Budi Santoso, F&B GM Senayan City, March 2026
```

**Why verbatim attributed quotes work:** they cannot be fabricated without exposure. They survive DD. They give the next operator permission to say yes.

## 7. Pilot offer slide (slide 10) — verbatim template

The B2B last slide is contractually-flavored. Specific and dated.

```
PILOT OFFER

Scope
  Venue: [Operator's specific venue name]
  Duration: 90 days from contract signing
  Coverage: [Specific scope — e.g. "All 47 food court tenants, full closed-loop"]
  Customer-facing: Wristband distribution to first 5,000 customers

Commercial
  Setup: Rp 0 (waived for pilot)
  Monthly platform fee: Rp 50 jt (waived months 1–2; charged month 3)
  GMV revenue share: 1% on closed-loop transactions only

Integration
  Day 0: Contract signed
  Day 1–7: Hardware install + tenant onboarding
  Day 8–12: Soft-launch with 5 priority tenants
  Day 13: Full pilot live across all 47 tenants
  Day 90: Joint review meeting + extension decision

Decision
  Required by: [DD-Mon-YYYY — exactly 4–6 weeks from pitch date]
  Next step: [Specific action — e.g. "Schedule 30-min site walkthrough by [DD-Mon]"]

[Lead's name]   [WhatsApp]   [Email]
```

**Critical:** the decision deadline + "next step" combination creates urgency without being pushy. It also signals you have other operators in the queue (because you do).

## 8. Indonesian-mode B2B specifics

| Element | Indonesian-mode behavior |
|---------|-------------------------|
| Currency | IDR primary throughout; no USD parenthetical needed (operator audience) |
| Hubungan | Mention named mutual contacts in opening / speaker notes |
| Compliance | BI license / OJK / Kominfo data residency surfaced on slide 8 |
| Trust signals | Named bank partners, named EO partners, named mall partners — logo-grid at bottom of slide 4 |
| Decision-making | Indonesian operators often consult internally for 2–4 weeks; the deadline must be realistic, not aggressive |
| Hierarchy | If pitching to multiple stakeholders (CEO + operations + finance), the deck must serve all three on different slides — slides 6 + 8 are for finance/ops; slides 1, 2, 9 are for CEO/strategy |

## 9. The 5 things B2B operators NEVER want to hear

Cut these from any B2B deck — they trigger immediate skepticism:

1. **"Disruption"** — operators don't want disruption; they want operational improvement they can defend to their board.
2. **"Revolutionary"** — sounds like change without runway; operators read it as "we'll break things."
3. **"Trust me on revenue"** — never; surface the audit trail.
4. **"Network effects will kick in"** — a VC concept; operators don't care about your network, they care about their venue.
5. **"Series A in 6 months"** — the operator doesn't care about your fundraising; mentioning it suggests you're not committed to making their venue work.

## 9.5. Dual-stakeholder framing (partnership pitches)

When the deck pitches a **partnership** (not just a sale) — i.e. both sides will share economics, brand co-presence, or operational responsibilities — the deck must visually frame BOTH parties on every body slide. This is different from a standard B2B sale, where the buyer is the only stakeholder visible.

### When dual-stakeholder framing is required

| Trigger | Example |
|---------|---------|
| Revenue split between platform + partner | Indusia POS × GO!Market (50:50 AI rev share) |
| Co-branded product (white-label, custom skin) | "GO!Cash POS" branded skin from Indusia base |
| Joint go-to-market (each party brings something the other can't) | Indusia brings tech, GO!Market brings tenants |
| Channel partnership where partner relays value to end customer | Salesforce ISV partnership model |
| Recoupable investment or "saya commit Rp X, kamu commit deadline Y" pattern | Partnership setup fee with revenue-share recoup |

### Visual patterns to use

**Pattern 1 — Match Framing (Slide 5 / Market):**
```
LEFT PANEL (primary color)        CENTER PANEL          RIGHT PANEL (accent color)
{Stakeholder A label}             "="                  {Stakeholder B label}
{A's strengths bullets ✓}         "MATCH"              {B's strengths bullets ✓}
                                   "perfect             
                                    collaboration"
```

**Pattern 2 — Two-Side Bridge (Slide 1 / Foreshadow):**
```
LEFT PANEL (secondary color)      CENTER PANEL          RIGHT PANEL (accent color)
{A's pains ❌}                    {Brand bridge        {B's pains ❌}
{What A needs}                     vertical arrow}     {What B needs}
                                   {3-layer summary}
```

**Pattern 3 — Revenue Split Visualization (Slide 7 / Money):**
```
HERO ECOSYSTEM TOTAL
        (large number)
─────────────────────────────────────
INDUSIA SHARE        |  GO!MARKET SHARE
{50% bar in primary} |  {50% bar in accent}
{revenue range}      |  {revenue range}
─────────────────────────────────────
3-LAYER MATH BREAKDOWN
{per-layer split per stakeholder}
```

**Pattern 4 — Dual Founder Cards (Cover slide 0 + Founder slide):**
```
{Founder A face badge      |     {Founder B face badge
 + name + role             |       + name + role
 + tagline}                |       + tagline}
(primary color card)       |     (accent color card)
```

### Hard rules

| Rule | Why |
|------|-----|
| Both stakeholders MUST be visually represented on cover, market, money, and closing slides | If only one side is visible, the other side reads as "vendor" rather than "partner" |
| Brand colors MUST signal stakeholder allegiance (primary = us, accent = partner OR vice versa) | Visual scanning collapses without color allegiance |
| Equal panel width (50/50 or 35/30/35) on dual-stakeholder slides | Asymmetric panels imply asymmetric power |
| Revenue split must be EXPLICIT — show both numbers, not just net | "Net new revenue" hides whose share is what |
| Founder/leader from BOTH sides should appear on cover + closing | Real-relationship signal vs. vendor relationship |

### Anti-patterns

| Anti-pattern | Why it fails |
|--------------|--------------|
| Cover only shows our brand (not partner brand) | Reads as "we're selling to them", not "we're partnering with them" |
| Money slide shows only "their net new revenue" | They want to see they're getting fair share, not just a number |
| Closing slide is one-sided (only our contact info) | Implies relationship is one-directional |
| Brand colors not allegiance-coded (random per slide) | Cognitive load + dilutes partner co-presence |

## 10. B2B validation checklist

Before approving a B2B-mode deck, verify:

- [ ] Slide 4 names ≥3 comparable venues (logos visible)
- [ ] Slide 6 has explicit ROI math + payback period + sensitivity
- [ ] Slide 7 is one named case study with before / after / verbatim quote
- [ ] Slide 8 names support SLA + escalation path + WhatsApp group
- [ ] Slide 10 has scope + commercial + timeline + dated deadline
- [ ] No "round size" language anywhere (this is a sale, not a fundraise)
- [ ] No "Series A" forecast (irrelevant to operator)
- [ ] All currency in IDR primary
- [ ] Source lines on every chart
- [ ] No banned vocabulary (Bahasa or English)
- [ ] **If partnership pitch:** dual-stakeholder framing visible on cover, market, money, closing slides (per §9.5)
- [ ] **If partnership pitch:** brand color allegiance consistent (primary = us, accent = partner)
- [ ] **If partnership pitch:** revenue split EXPLICIT — both shares visible, not just net

If any unchecked, send back to relevant upstream skill.
