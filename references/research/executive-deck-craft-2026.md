## 0. The One Sentence

> **Lead with the decision, not the data. Make every slide one idea, stated as a conclusion, understood in 5 seconds, with the visual doing the work and your mouth doing the words.**

If a generation choice ever conflicts, resolve it toward that sentence.

---

## 1. The Decision-First Architecture (overrides "build-up" narration)

Executives do **not** want your analytical journey. They want your destination, then the option to drill down. Invert the natural order:

| ❌ Traditional (what we instinctively build) | ✅ Decision-First (what executives want) |
|---|---|
| Background → Process → Data → Analysis → Recommendation | **Recommendation → Impact → Risk → Supporting evidence (only if asked)** |

**The canonical opening sequence** (adapt slide roles, keep the order):
1. **Decision slide** — exactly what you want + the expected outcome. No preamble. (e.g. *"Request: £4M to reduce customer churn 23% within 18 months."* — an 11-word slide that flips a CFO from "let's circle back" to "walk me through the numbers.")
2. **Impact slide** — what happens if they say yes, quantified in revenue / cost / risk / strategic position.
3. **Risk slide** — what could go wrong + mitigation. Executives *always* think downside; pre-empt it or look unprepared.
4. **Evidence slides (≈3)** — only data that helps them say yes.
5. **Appendix / backup** — methodology, deep analysis, detail-on-demand.

Leading with the recommendation changes the room's posture: instead of *waiting to find out what you want*, the executive starts *evaluating whether to approve*. You shift from "presenter explaining" to "advisor proposing."

## 2. The Minto Pyramid Principle (the governing framework)

Developed by Barbara Minto at McKinsey; the standard at MBB firms, investment banks, and the Fortune 500.

- **Think bottom-up, present top-down.** State the answer first, then group the supporting arguments, then the evidence beneath each.
- **Rule of Three.** Exactly **3 supporting points** under the recommendation. Working memory holds ~3–4 chunks; >3 reads as a list to wade through; <3 feels thin. *If you have 5 points, you haven't synthesized enough — group them.*
- **MECE** (Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive): supporting points must not overlap ("saves money" + "reduces cost" = overlap) and together must cover the ground (no gaps that invite "but what about…?"). Test before finalizing: *Is there overlap? If they accept all 3, do they accept the answer? What's the strongest objection, and is it addressed?*

### SCQA / SCR for the opening and the exec-summary slide
- **SCQA**: **S**ituation (shared context they already know) → **C**omplication (what changed; creates tension) → **Q**uestion (what must we address) → **A**nswer (your recommendation).
- **SCR** (for the one-slide executive summary): **S**ituation 10–15% → **C**omplication 15–20% → **R**esolution **60–70%**. The Resolution dominates because that's what executives care about.

## 3. Action Titles (assertion headlines) — non-negotiable

Every slide title is a **complete sentence stating the takeaway**, not a topic label. This is the single highest-leverage technique.

| ❌ Topic title | ✅ Action title |
|---|---|
| Market Overview | German market growing 12% annually, 3× faster than US |
| Competitive Analysis | We outperform competitors on 4 of 6 key purchase criteria |
| Financial Projections | Acquisition generates 15% IRR with conservative assumptions |

**The 5 golden rules for action titles:**
1. One clear **sentence** with a **verb**. No fragments, colons, or ellipses.
2. Lead with the **insight**, not the data source ("Our margins improved 5%", not "Analysis of margin data").
3. **Active voice + strong verbs** to imply movement ("Customer satisfaction *drives* retention").
4. **Quantify** when possible — numbers boost credibility and scannability.
5. **≤15 words, ≤2 lines.** If it won't fit, you're cramming >1 idea → split into two slides.

**The Billboard Test**: a partner should flip through *titles only* and understand the entire argument. If any title is a topic, the chain breaks. (Action titles give a measured ~15% recall lift at 48h and let executives scan ~3× faster.)

## 4. Cognitive Load — how much is allowed on a slide

- **One idea per slide.** Each slide delivers exactly one insight tied to one business outcome (revenue, risk, cost, timeline, competitive position). Two points = two slides. A presenter who clusters ideas dilutes all of them.
- **Word budget.** Treat the slide like a one-page executive summary: **under ~500 words is for a whole document** — a single slide should be far leaner. Replace explanations with short **interpretive statements**: *"This variance increases exposure."* / *"This trend improves margin stability."*
- **Visual density is judged before reading.** Executives decide whether something is worth reading *in the first ~2 seconds*, on density alone. A wall of text — even technically one page — gets filed under "work for later" (i.e. never). A dense visual signals unclear thinking; a sprawling bullet list signals no prioritization.
- **Pacing.** Plan ~**1 minute per slide** in delivery; a 20-minute talk ≈ ~20 slides. A disciplined slide can be summarized verbally in **≤20 seconds** when the meeting compresses.
- **Time economics.** A CEO at ~£1.5–2M/yr is worth ~£750–1,000/hour. A 10-page brief that takes 30 min costs ~£375–500 of their time *before they know what you want*. Brevity is respect, not dumbing-down.

## 5. Visual-vs-Text & Data Visualization

Executives **respond to synthesis faster than to narration.** They will not reconstruct meaning from raw data — they expect interpretation: *what does this mean for the business?*

- **The 5-Second Rule**: the best executive chart is understood in **under 5 seconds with no verbal explanation.** If a visual needs explaining, it's the wrong visual.
- **Show the interpretation, not the dataset.** A chart with the action title *as* its interpretation > a chart the reader must decode.
- **Match chart type to intent:**

  | Goal | Chart |
  |---|---|
  | Numerical comparison | Bar / column |
  | Proportion / share | Pie / donut |
  | Trend over time | Line / area |
  | Process / conversion | Flow / funnel |
  | Hierarchy / structure | Tree / sunburst |

- **Avoid "AI slop"** — cluttered clip art, generic stock, random decoration. Use clean, professional KPI dashboards, comparison charts, and structured arrangements. (Construction details live in [`../deck-infographic-system.md`](../deck-infographic-system.md); image-prompt craft in [`nano-banana-pro-prompting-2026.md`](./nano-banana-pro-prompting-2026.md).)
- **Cognitive-bias caution** (arXiv): readers use heuristics that produce "visualization mirages" — truncated axes, misleading area encodings, and cherry-picked ranges are read as fact. Design so the *correct* reading is the *effortless* reading: zero-baseline bars, honest scales, one encoding per variable, the takeaway pre-stated in the title.
- **Source every number.** Citations in 8–9pt at the bottom of the slide / under the chart. At MBB this is not optional — unsourced data loses credibility instantly.

## 6. Narrative Arc — making it land emotionally

Numbers impress; **stories persuade.** Decision-makers are rational *and* emotional; the decision to back something is evidence + intuition, and intuition is sparked by emotion. A narrative arc gives context to facts, purpose to numbers, humanity to the pitch.

**Three stages (keep momentum, not theatrics):**
1. **Beginning — exposition.** Anchor in today's reality: who you are, why we're here, what's wrong with the world today.
2. **Middle — tension & breakthrough.** Make the pain tangible and urgent; introduce the solution as the **inflection point** that shifts the story from frustration to possibility (not a dry feature list).
3. **End — resolution & vision.** Proof points + validation, then cast the **future state** that only becomes possible *with the audience's support*. A pitch without forward resolution strands the listener in the present.

**Humanize the data — personification.** Instead of *"20% of users churn,"* say *"one in five people walk away disappointed, never to return."* Same market-size graph, but only the narrative version makes the room lean in. Frame the **"why" behind the "what."**

The arc and the Decision-First structure are not in conflict: SCR *is* the arc compressed (Complication = tension); the modular, headline-first structure simply makes the arc **interruption-tolerant** — each point stands alone when an executive jumps in.

## 7. Credibility Killers — what makes executives stop listening

Executives form an opinion within ~30 seconds. Each of these signals "not ready for senior conversations":

1. **Burying the recommendation** under context. If the ask appears on slide 11 of 14, you've asked them to hold attention through 10 slides they didn't request. → *State the recommendation in the first 30 seconds.*
2. **Hedge-word epidemic** — "I think," "maybe," "might," "possibly," "perhaps," "kind of." Every hedge halves perceived conviction. → State positions as positions: *"I recommend Option B."*
3. **Over-explaining before asked.** Answering objections nobody raised plants doubts and signals anxiety. → Present the case, stop, let questions emerge.
4. **Reading the slides.** Executives read faster than you speak; reading aloud signals you don't know your content. → Slides are visual aids, not a script.
5. **Treating Q&A as attack.** Defensiveness/over-justification reads as discomfort with scrutiny. → Tough questions = engagement = being taken seriously.
6. **Technical jargon overuse / outcome misalignment.** Untranslated jargon and recommendations not mapped to risk/cost/capacity/strategy read as incomplete thinking. → Translate every technical point into a business implication.

## 8. The "So What" Test & Deck Length

- **"So what?" on every element.** Point at anything on a slide; if you can't connect it to the action title, cut it or move it to backup. Most decks lose **30–40% of slides** this way — and get dramatically more effective.
- **Length**: main deck **8–12 slides** + appendix. The number isn't sacred; the rule is *every slide must help them decide.* Executives who "prefer detail" prefer detail *available on request* — keep it in backup; they rarely ask, but they respect that it exists.
- **Standalone test**: consulting-grade decks must read correctly **without the presenter** (a board member reads the PDF Friday night). Titles + visuals must carry the argument alone.

## 9. Generation Checklist (apply during gen + validate)

Slide-level:
- [ ] Action title is a complete sentence stating a conclusion (≤15 words, ≤2 lines)?
- [ ] Exactly **one idea** per slide?
- [ ] Does every element support the title? ("so what" passes?)
- [ ] Understandable in **≤5 seconds** (visual) / **≤30 seconds** (whole slide)?
- [ ] Every number sourced (8–9pt)?

Deck-level:
- [ ] Opens with the **decision/recommendation**, not background?
- [ ] Supporting points are **3, MECE**?
- [ ] Main deck **8–12 slides**; detail pushed to appendix?
- [ ] Reads correctly **without** a presenter (billboard test on titles)?
- [ ] One clear **ask** with a deadline at the end ("Approval to proceed by Friday COB")?
- [ ] **Zero hedge words**; positions stated as positions?
- [ ] Visual-first: synthesis/charts over text walls; no "AI slop"?
